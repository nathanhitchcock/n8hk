After removing major blockers, the next risk is drift. Teams without cadence fall back into reactive mode.

Step 4 is where you install rhythm: a practical, lightweight system that keeps priorities, decisions, and follow-through aligned.

## Objective

Build a predictable operating cadence that improves execution reliability without adding meeting bloat.

## Core Cadence Design

Your cadence needs three layers:

- Weekly execution loop: plan, unblock, review.
- Monthly calibration loop: outcomes, risks, resourcing.
- Quarterly direction loop: strategy alignment and tradeoffs.

If these loops are unclear, teams default to urgency over importance.

## Weekly Operating Loop

Use this structure every week:

1. Priority lock (what must ship this week).
2. Midweek unblock (what is stuck, who owns escalation).
3. End-week review (what moved, what slipped, why).

Document decisions in one location. If decisions are not visible, they are not durable.

## Meeting Rules That Keep Quality High

- Every meeting has a decision intent.
- Every decision has an owner and due date.
- Every recurring meeting is reviewed every 6 weeks for value.

Kill meetings that only transfer anxiety.

## What to Track in Cadence

Track only leading signals that help action:

- Priority completion rate.
- Blocked work aging.
- Decision latency.
- Commitment reliability.

These are better for operations than vanity output counts.

## Artifacts to Ship

By end of Step 4, publish:

1. Weekly operating agenda template.
2. Decision log standard.
3. Escalation protocol and response window.

## Done Criteria

This step is complete when:

- The team can run the weekly loop without manager heroics.
- Priorities remain stable through the week unless explicit tradeoffs are made.
- Stakeholders trust update quality because the rhythm is consistent.

Cadence is the operating system of a high-performing team.
