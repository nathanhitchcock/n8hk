Step 3 is the ownership moment.

Analysis is useful, but leadership growth happens when someone commits to one recommendation.

## Objective

Choose one option, justify it clearly, and define who owns execution and follow-through.

## Recommendation Structure

Use this format:

1. Recommended option: "I recommend Option X."
2. Why this option: key tradeoffs and constraints considered.
3. What we are accepting: explicit downsides and known risks.
4. What support is needed: approvals, budget, cross-team decisions.
5. Next actions and owner: who does what by when.

This turns decision quality into execution quality.

## Accountability Checklist

A recommendation is incomplete without ownership.

Define:

- Decision owner.
- Execution owner.
- Success metric.
- Review checkpoint date.
- Escalation trigger if the plan drifts.

If these are missing, the recommendation is just a narrative.

## Common Failure Modes

Watch for:

- Recommendation hedging: "Any of these could work." 
- Hidden risk: risks are known but omitted to get approval faster.
- Unowned execution: no clear owner for next actions.

These patterns create delay and decision debt.

## Manager Coaching Prompts

- "What are you recommending, specifically?"
- "What downside are you explicitly accepting?"
- "Who owns execution and when do we review results?"

Coaching should increase commitment quality, not replace it.

<Callout title="Decision Quality Standard">
  <p>
    A strong recommendation is clear, accountable, and falsifiable. If it fails, the team should be able to explain why and adapt quickly.
  </p>
</Callout>

## Done Criteria

This framework is complete when:

- A single recommendation is made.
- Tradeoffs and risks are explicit.
- Ownership and checkpoints are defined.
- Execution starts without ambiguity.

That is how 1-3-1 builds critical thinking and accountability over time.
