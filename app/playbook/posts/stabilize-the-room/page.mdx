When you inherit a team, your first problem is rarely talent. It is usually volatility.

People are unsure what matters, who decides what, and whether speaking plainly is safe. Before strategy, before process optimization, before ambitious roadmaps, stabilize the room.

## Objective

Create enough calm and trust in the first two weeks so the team can execute without guessing.

## What to Do in Week 1

- Run a focused listening tour: one-on-ones with every team member and key stakeholder.
- Ask the same five questions every time:
  - What is working?
  - What is slowing us down?
  - Where are decisions getting stuck?
  - What are we pretending is fine?
  - What would you fix first?
- Publish a short summary: what you heard, what you need to verify, what you will do first.

## What to Do in Week 2

- Set communication rules (how updates happen, where decisions live, response expectations).
- Define immediate priorities: top 3 outcomes for the next 30 days.
- Clarify ownership for current projects and unresolved decisions.
- Create one predictable check-in rhythm for the team.

<Callout title="Guardrails">
  <ul>
    <li>Do not reorganize yet.</li>
    <li>Do not launch ten initiatives at once.</li>
    <li>Do not promise certainty you do not have.</li>
  </ul>
  <p>Early overcorrection destroys trust. Early clarity builds it.</p>
</Callout>

## Artifacts to Ship

By end of week 2, publish these four artifacts:

1. Team snapshot (what is true right now).
2. Priority list (top 3 outcomes, with owners).
3. Decision log location (single source of truth).
4. Operating cadence (weekly rhythm and meeting intent).

## Done Criteria

This step is complete when:

- Team members can repeat priorities without guessing.
- Ownership is obvious for active work.
- Stakeholders know when and how decisions happen.
- Escalations drop because expectations are clearer.

Stability is not slowness. Stability is what allows speed to compound.
