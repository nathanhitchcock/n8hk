Most bad decisions start with a blurry problem.

Step 1 of 1-3-1 is simple: define one clear problem statement.

If the problem is vague, the options and recommendation will be weak no matter how polished they look.

## Objective

Frame the issue precisely so downstream option analysis and recommendations are grounded in reality.

## Problem Statement Template

Write the problem in 4 lines:

1. What is happening?
2. Why does it matter now?
3. What constraint or risk makes this urgent?
4. What does success look like if solved?

If you cannot answer all four, you are not ready for Step 2.

## Quality Bar

A strong problem statement is:

- Specific, not generic.
- Evidence-based, not emotional.
- Bounded, not trying to solve everything at once.

Weak example:

- "Delivery is bad."

Stronger example:

- "Deployment frequency dropped 40% over 6 weeks, and two customer commitments are now at risk within this quarter."

## Coaching Prompts for Managers

When someone brings a vague issue, ask:

- "What exactly is failing?"
- "What evidence supports that?"
- "What scope are we solving in this decision?"

Do not move to option generation until the problem is precise.

## Common Failure Modes

Watch for:

- Bundled problems: multiple issues mixed into one statement.
- Missing urgency: no reason this needs action now.
- Unclear success condition: no measurable definition of solved.

If these are present, rewrite the problem before continuing.

<Callout title="Ready for Step 2?">
  <p>
    Move to Step 2 only when the team can agree on the same problem statement without debate.
  </p>
</Callout>

## Done Criteria

This step is complete when:

- The problem is specific and bounded.
- Urgency and constraints are explicit.
- Success condition is clear.

Step 1 creates the foundation. Step 2 is where tradeoff quality is won or lost.
