One of the first lessons I learned scaling a small team to dozens of people was that not all work measures the same. An interruption in routine task work is annoying. An interruption in knowledge work—designing systems, writing code, solving problems—is corrosive.

## The Math of Interruption

When someone interrupts you mid-flow, they're not just asking for 10 minutes of your time. They're asking for 10 minutes plus the context rebuild time. For complex work, that rebuild time is often 20-30 minutes.

Research shows knowledge workers spend an average of 23 minutes recovering from an interruption. But observation suggests it's worse when you're in deep, creative problem-solving. You're not just recovering—you're trying to remember where you were *theoretically*, not just where you were *practically*.

## What Gets Lost

The real cost isn't the time map. It's the quality of decisions made once you're partially-recovered.

I watched this happen repeatedly: someone would interrupt an engineer mid-architecture planning, ask a quick question, get an answer, leave satisfied. The engineer would then try to resume, but they'd lost the thread of their thinking. They'd make a decision that *seemed* reasonable, but it lacked the nuance of their original thinking. Six weeks later, we're refactoring.

The initial interruption cost 10 minutes. The poor decision cost weeks.

## Setting Boundaries Without Being Difficult

The solution isn't to lock engineers away in caves. Teams need communication. The trick is *batching* communication effectively.

What actually works:

- **Office hours**: "I'm available for questions 3-4 PM." This turns unlimited interruptions into scheduled, grouped conversations.
- **Async-first default**: Slack message instead of ping. Document decisions in shared places instead of explaining individually.
- **Written decision-making**: Small decisions get documented in a shared channel or doc. People see the reasoning, propose changes asynchronously.

The counter-intuitive part? This actually *increases* communication quality. When you have to write something down, you think more carefully.

## The Operator's Role

As your team grows, protecting deep work time becomes part of your job. Not by being authoritarian about it, but by:

1. **Modeling it**: Take your own deep work seriously. Block time on your calendar. Don't interrupt others unless it's urgent.
2. **Making it normal**: Talk about context switching in retrospectives. Celebrate when projects come in on time because people had uninterrupted focus.
3. **Building systems**: Implement patterns that naturally batch communication (office hours, async docs, decision logs).
4. **Being explicit**: Tell people "this project needs 3 focused days—let's protect that time."

The best outcome is when the team internalizes this themselves. Junior engineers start respecting senior engineers' deep work time without being asked. People default to async communication.

That's when you know the culture is actually shifting.
