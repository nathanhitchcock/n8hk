It is 4:57 and the customer has escalated.

## The Escalation Moment

Now you are pulled into executive triage with Sales, CX, Product, and Engineering - all huddled together trying to explain what happened.

The first question hits: "Why has nothing been done to resolve this issue?"

You pause. Nothing?

"We have been on a bridge about this all week..."

## Where It Broke

The team (and you) are confused.

There were meetings, peer reviews, architects pulled in, fixes already in progress, and pull requests submitted - work *was* happening, just not where the customer could see it.

There was only one thing missing: the external update.

And that is where everything broke.

## The Three-Element Update

If you have ever wondered why your updates are met with pushback, it is usually not about the work itself. It is because one of these three ingredients is missing:

- Current Status - What have we currently done?
- Next Steps - What are the next steps?
- Next Update - When will the next update be?

## Failure Modes

If you do not give at least this information during an update, your customer will ask follow-ups or fill in the missing pieces themselves - and it is rarely filled in your favor.

The failure modes make sense:

- No current status? -> "No work is being done."
- No next steps? -> "There is no plan."
- No next update? -> "We need to escalate."

## Narrative Debt

Like technical debt, this creates **Narrative Debt** - when work is happening in the background, but the story is not keeping up.

## Why Teams Stay Silent

Here is the deeper issue: engineers are trained to avoid being wrong.

We do not want to give false information, so the default becomes giving no information until we have figured it all out. This prevents backlash if information is given prematurely, but it also leaves customers in the dark until a perfect answer is identified.

But silence does not create safety - it creates narratives. And those narratives escalate a lot faster than reality.

## What Customers Actually Need

Most customers are not looking for perfection - okay, maybe some are - but most just want to be kept in the loop as things progress.

If you include these three elements, you are giving your customer a complete story, even if no perceived progress is being made. Some information is better than no information.

If you or your team is dealing with pressure, escalations, or constant follow-ups...

Do not just look at the work itself - look at the updates.
